What this shows

This tool represents Statistics Canada's published 2024 estimates for the ten provinces, each shown against the national rate with a 95% confidence interval. It performs no additional modeling. Access to primary and specialist care is usually reported as a single provincial number, which hides how much of the spread between provinces is real rather than survey noise, and how unevenly access falls across age and gender within a province.

In Nova Scotia, two differences are significant against the national rate: residents are less likely to have a regular health care provider (77.1% against 81.0%) and more likely to report an unmet health care need (25.0% against 22.4%). Within the province, the youngest adults carry most of that gap. Unmet need runs 32.8% among adults aged 18 to 44 against 14.6% among those 65 and over, and provider attachment is lowest in that same younger group. These within-province age differences are not formally tested here, so they are a pattern to watch rather than an established finding.

What is tested. The only significance test in the source is each province against the national rate (bootstrapped, p<0.05), and it is not corrected for the fact that ten provinces are tested per indicator. Province-to-province, age-to-age, and gender differences are not tested; confidence intervals are shown so differences can be judged, but overlapping intervals do not by themselves establish a difference and non-overlap is a conservative signal.

Reading the color. In "estimate magnitude" mode, fill encodes the value only and carries no judgment about whether high is good or bad; that depends on the indicator. In "difference from national" mode, fill marks direction and significance against the national rate.

Coverage. Percentage estimates are near-complete across strata. Suppressed or unavailable cells are hatched and shown as "n/a" and omitted from comparisons. An E flag marks estimates to use with caution.

Gender. Men and Women are a modified two-category gender variable (not sex); non-binary respondents are redistributed under Statistics Canada confidentiality rules.
